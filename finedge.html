01 — The Problem

Users were interested — so why weren't they buying?

FinEdge had strong top-of-funnel metrics. Users were discovering products, spending time on pages, and adding items to cart. But somewhere between the product detail page and checkout, a significant portion were abandoning. The business knew there was a problem. No one knew exactly why.

I was brought in to diagnose the friction, hypothesise solutions, and ship a redesign — all within a 10-week sprint cycle aligned to a major app update.

The Key Question
"We have the intent. We're losing the action. What's breaking between wanting and buying?"
02 — Research

Finding the real friction

I ran three parallel research tracks: session recordings via Hotjar to watch real abandonment patterns, five moderated usability tests with target users, and a heuristic audit of the existing PDP against Nielsen's principles.

Three patterns emerged consistently: users couldn't quickly assess product trustworthiness (no visible reviews above the fold), the size/variant selector was buried below a long description, and the checkout CTA was competing visually with four other actions at the same hierarchy level.

03 — Process

From diagnosis to decision

01 — Discover
Mapping the full journey
Session recordings, user interviews, and funnel analytics to understand where exactly intent was being lost and what users were trying to do at each step.
02 — Define
Prioritising the friction points
Ranked findings by impact × effort. Three high-impact, low-effort changes emerged: trust signal placement, variant selector position, and CTA visual hierarchy.
03 — Design
Progressive disclosure approach
Restructured the PDP around user intent: see → validate → configure → buy. Each section answers one question before the user has to ask it.
04 — Test & Ship
A/B test + iterative rollout
Ran a 2-week A/B test on 20% of users before full rollout. Iterated on one edge case around variant selection before the final ship.
04 — Solution

Rebuilding the path to purchase

The redesigned PDP follows a strict information hierarchy — hero image, trust signals (rating + review count + badges), variant selector, then a single dominant CTA. Every element that competed with the primary action was removed or demoted.

I introduced a sticky bottom bar that followed users as they scrolled — surfacing the CTA and price without interrupting their evaluation phase. This alone accounted for the majority of the conversion lift.

Key Insight
"Users weren't confused — they were anxious. Every design decision became about reducing anxiety, not just reducing steps."
05 — Results

Numbers that moved

The redesign went live in Q3 2024. Within 4 weeks of full rollout, every primary metric had improved. The team was particularly pleased with the basket size improvement — an unexpected byproduct of surfacing related products at the right moment in the purchase flow.

The pattern library built for this project was subsequently adopted as part of a wider design system initiative, saving an estimated 3 weeks of design work on the next major feature.
